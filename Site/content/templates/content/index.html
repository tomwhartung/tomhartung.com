Decades before Twain's observations, Alexander Hamilton saw the same sort of predictable, safe, conventional corn-pone thinking:

Men often oppose a thing, merely because they have had no agency in planning it, or because it may have been planned by those whom they dislike.
 —  Alexander Hamilton, as Publius in Federalist No. 70, The Executive Department Further Considered, March 18, 1788.

A political opinion can be non-corn-pone by simply being both liberal and conservative simultaneously — or by being neither liberal nor conservative.

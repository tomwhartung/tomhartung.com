The Introduction

Published in 2016, the book Deep Work: Rules for Focused Success in a Distracted World by Cal Newport is bold and daring in its view of today's popular culture.

Dr. Newport, who earned his Doctorate from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and now is an Associate Professor at Georgetown University, sees most people as being on automatic pilot. Most of us are bobbing through life, lost in shallow social networks when we could be more mindful of our opportunities, taking control of our lives, and producing something of real value.

Deep Work

It's essential to read the book's introduction, because it is where he defines deep work.

Deep Work: Professional work performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit. These efforts create new value, improve your skill, and are hard to duplicate.
 — Deep Work by Cal Newport, p. 3.

Cal Newport considers the work of people such as Carl Jung, Mark Twain, Woody Allen, Bill Gates, and J. K. Rowling as examples of the value of being able to work deeply.

Given the benefits, we would think that bosses would encourage deep work. This rarely happens, though, due to what Dr. Newport calls the metric black hole. Because the results of deep work are frequently invisible, it is difficult to measure the results.

Shallow Work

The book cites current authors such as Nicholas Carr, William Powers, John Freeman, and Alex Soojung-Kin Pang who have written recently about how being constantly connected online is ruining our ability to concentrate on difficult problems.

Shallow Work: Noncognitively demanding, logistical-style tasks, often performed while distracted. These efforts tend to not create much new value in the world and are easy to replicate.
 — Deep Work by Cal Newport, p. 6.

Because shallow work does not create much new value  and is easy to replicate,  it is also easy to outsource, automate — and even eliminate.

The Deep Work Hypothesis: The ability to perform deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable in our economy. As a consequence, the few who cultivate this skill, and then make it the core of their working life, will thrive.
 — Deep Work by Cal Newport, p. 14.

The Book

The rest of book is in two parts. Part 1 defines what deep work is, and Part 2 details how to do more of it.
